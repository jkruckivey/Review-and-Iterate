The Same Business, Two Methods

Understanding Cash vs. Accrual Accounting

It's time to explore two different scenarios and compare them to determine which method works best.

Introduction

Scenario 1: December Consulting Project

Let's explore how timing affects revenue recognition

Timeline:
December 15
You complete a $10,000 consulting project
December 31
Year-end (time to prepare financial statements)
January 10
Client pays you $10,000

Scenario 1: Comparing the Methods

How does each method handle the same transaction?

Cash Accounting View:
December Revenue:
$0
(no cash received yet)
January Revenue:
$10,000
(cash received)
Accrual Accounting View:
December Revenue:
$10,000
(earned when service delivered)
January Revenue:
$0
(cash collected, no new economic event)

Scenario 1: The Verdict

Which method gives a better picture of December's performance?
Accrual!
You did the work in December. That's when you created and delivered value and earned the revenue. The fact that the client paid in January is just a cash timing issue—it doesn't change the economic reality of when you performed the service.

Scenario 2: Prepaid Rent (Expense Matching)

Now let's see how expenses are handled differently

Timeline:
January 1
You pay $12,000 cash for 12 months of office rent (January-December)

Scenario 2: Comparing the Methods

How should the rent expense be recognized?

Cash Accounting View:
January Expense:
$12,000
(entire amount paid in January)
Feb-Dec Expense:
$0
each month (no cash paid)
Accrual Accounting View:
Every Month:
$1,000
expense ($12,000 ÷ 12 months)

Scenario 2: The Verdict

Which method gives a better picture of monthly performance?
Accrual!
You're using the office space all year, not just in January. Matching $1,000 of rent expense to each month reflects the economic reality that you're consuming the space benefit evenly throughout the year.
