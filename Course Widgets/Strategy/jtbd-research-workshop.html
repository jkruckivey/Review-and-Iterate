Step 1: Study Customer Observation

Read the following observation from a pre-surgery consultation at an orthopedic clinic. Take notes on what you observe about the customer's situation, concerns, and current solutions.

Observational Research: Pre-Surgery Consultation
Context: Margaret (62) sits in the orthopedic surgeon's office reviewing knee replacement surgery options. Her daughter accompanies her, taking notes.
What We Observed:
Margaret: "My main concern is knowing if something goes wrong after I leave the hospital. With my hip replacement 5 years ago, I had to go back three times because of pain, and they couldn't tell if it was normal healing or infection. Each visit was $200 copay, plus my daughter had to take off work to drive me."
Surgeon's Response:
"We can offer a smart implant that wirelessly transmits data about your recovery - how much weight you're putting on it, swelling indicators, temperature changes. I can see this on my dashboard and call you if anything looks concerning."
Margaret's Reaction:
"So I wouldn't have to wonder if I should call? And I wouldn't have to go in unless there's actually a problem? That would save my daughter from missing work, and honestly, I'd sleep better knowing you're monitoring it. How much extra is the smart implant?"
Surgeon's Explanation:
"For you, there's no additional cost - your insurance covers it because it reduces readmission rates. The hospital actually prefers it because Medicare penalizes them for readmissions within 30 days. Traditional implants have a 12% readmission rate; smart implants reduce that to 4%."
Decision:
Margaret chose the smart implant. When asked why: "I'm hiring it to give me peace of mind and save unnecessary trips to the doctor. I trust my surgeon to watch the data and tell me if there's a real problem."
