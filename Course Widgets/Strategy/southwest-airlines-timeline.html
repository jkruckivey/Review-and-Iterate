Phase 1 Building the Strategy (1971–1989)

Strategic Elements

  • Short-haul, point-to-point routes
  • Single aircraft type (Boeing 737)
  • Low fares, no frills
  • Fun, employee-centric culture
  • Quick turnarounds at gate

Performance

~$15

Key Insight

Southwest established a coherent, differentiated strategy where every element reinforced the others. Single aircraft type enabled quick turnarounds, which enabled point-to-point efficiency, which enabled low fares. This wasn't about being "cheap"—it was about being different.

Phase 2 Strategy-Driven Success (1990–2010)

Strategic Elements

  • Boeing 737 range improvements
  • Route expansion across US
  • Maintained core differentiation
  • No hub-and-spoke, no assigned seats
  • Free checked bags (differentiation)

Critical Insight

Much of Southwest's profitability surge came from Boeing 737 improvements—an external factor that disproportionately benefited their single-aircraft model. The strategy didn't change; the environment became more favorable. This raises a question: How much was strategic genius vs. environmental luck?

Phase 3 Execution & Extension (2011–2019)

Strategic Elements

  • AirTran acquisition (2011)
  • Wright Amendment repeal (2014)
  • Continued route expansion
  • International flights added
  • Strategy largely maintained

Key Insight

Southwest extended its successful strategy while competitors converged on similar models (unbundled pricing, ancillary fees). The differentiation that once set Southwest apart was becoming less distinctive. Signs of strategic exhaustion were emerging even before COVID.

  • Profitability dropped significantly

The Critical Question

Is Southwest's shift toward industry norms (assigned seating, fees) necessary adaptation or dangerous drift? This is exactly the kind of question the persistence framework helps you analyze. Every strategy eventually reaches its limits—the question is what comes next.

Framework Application

Southwest's journey illustrates how even exceptional strategies face limits. Use the drift vs. adaptation framework to analyze: Are Southwest's changes solving the real problem or the apparent problem?
