Patterns, Trajectories, and Closure

The three concepts that make up aesthetic coherence

Step 1
Patterns
Basic units of meaning that audiences already know from prior experience. The director or designer does not create them from scratch; they count on the audience bringing them into the room.
Step 2
Trajectories
What patterns combine to form. When two or more patterns are juxtaposed, they point in a direction and generate expectations about what is likely to happen next.
Step 3
Closure
The moment expectations are met or surprised. When a surprising closure feels right, it produces an emotional reaction. We get chills. We say: "I did not see that coming."
In the opening of The Sixth Sense
Patterns
Two patterns, drawn from the audience's prior experience of films and stories:
"There's someone in the house" "The proud shall stumble and fall"
Trajectory
Juxtaposed, the two patterns point toward a likely outcome: whoever is in the house will be the instrument by which the proud hero is laid low.
Closure
Expected
A former patient confronts Malcolm, accuses him of failing, and shoots him. The expectation is met. Short-term closure.
Surprising
The film's ending causes audiences to re-evaluate everything they thought was happening. A closure that surprises and yet makes sense.
Coherence is the overall quality of a made thing whose patterns, trajectories, and closures fit together without loose ends, in a way the audience recognizes as exactly right.
