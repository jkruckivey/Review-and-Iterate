We'll take a deep dive into the processes involved in innovation. We'll try to understand where great new ideas come from and how managers can create the conditions that often lead to ideas that are original and valuable. We'll discover that innovation processes are unlike other business processes in important ways, and that they need to be managed differently.

We'll look at how customers, users, and other "outsiders" can be usefully involved in innovation. This is a relatively new development in our understanding of innovation. We've come to realize that customers, users and others, have always had more to do with innovation than we thought, and that it is often in our best interests as innovation managers to facilitate their involvement. Design Thinking and crowdsourcing extend this idea in fascinating ways.

We'll focus on the challenges of taking innovations to market. Coming up with a great new idea does not guarantee its acceptance by customers. Business history is littered with the carcasses of better ideas that did not catch on or win out. We'll explore the surprisingly complex factors that cause ideas to spread (or not spread), and how we, as managers, might influence those factors.

We'll look at "creative industries" where both the process and the product or service are valued for their creative content, such as fashion, design, movies, video games, music and other similar businesses. The importance of this category is increasing, as products valued for functionality often also need to be cool or beautiful. An appreciation for such qualities is finding its way into businesses that have historically been mostly about practical functionality and utilitarian value.

We'll explore the implications for innovation of the rise of AI, especially Generative AI. Unlike Analytic AI (used to classify, predict, diagnose), Gen AI creates new content and can be applied to design, writing, graphics, and code. It holds potential to supercharge ideation by harnessing valuable randomness, supporting iterative processes, and helping us recognize value in unfamiliar forms. This is an emerging area, but already we can see new paths to important value creation.
