Scenario: The Forma Chair

Apply what you have learned about coherence, timing, and the manager's role

The Situation

Meridian, a design-forward furniture company, has just launched the Forma chair. It was developed by a celebrated designer, Elena Voss, who was given complete creative autonomy throughout the process. The result is striking: an unconventional form that does not resemble any chair currently on the market.

Before launch, Meridian's market research team assessed the Forma against the bestselling ergonomic office chairs of the past three years. Their forecast was weak. They projected modest sales based on the fact that the Forma did not share the features, proportions, or aesthetic language of those successful products. Leadership proceeded anyway, trusting Voss.

Six months after launch, sales are quiet. The chair has received almost no press. A small number of buyers are passionate about it. The majority of consumers seem unaware it exists. You have been brought in as a consultant to diagnose what went wrong.

What is your initial diagnosis of the Forma's poor reception?
New Information

You dig deeper. You arrange for a small group of design-literate consumers to spend time with the Forma before giving their reactions. Their response is striking: once they understand what Voss was trying to do, once someone explains the thinking behind the form, they are enthusiastic. Several say it is unlike anything they have seen, but that it makes complete sense once you see it.

This confirms your suspicion: the Forma is not incoherent. The parts fit together. There are no loose ends. The problem is that most customers have not yet been equipped to see what makes it special.

Given this, what do you recommend to Meridian's leadership?
What This Scenario Illustrates

The Forma scenario is designed to surface two of the most common mistakes in managing creative products. The first is using category comparisons to assess coherence. The market research team's forecast was not wrong because they were bad at their jobs; it was wrong because category comparisons are the wrong tool for predicting the success of a coherent creative product. As your instructor notes, it is not about whether a thing resembles something else, but about how well put together this particular thing is.

The second mistake is conflating a perception problem with a coherence problem. The Forma's quiet reception was not evidence of incoherence. It was evidence that customers had not yet been equipped to see the patterns from which its coherence was constructed. That is a marketing problem, not a design problem. And it is a problem the manager must solve, not the designer.

Notice also where the decision about commercial viability belongs: with leadership, not with Voss. The creative's job is to defend coherence. The manager's job is to decide how far out on the cliff the company can afford to go.
