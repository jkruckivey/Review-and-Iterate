Measurable gains, fast
When McNerney introduced Six Sigma at 3M in 2000, results were swift and tangible. The company trimmed 10% of its workforce and operating margins climbed from 17% to 23% in just four years. By conventional measures, it was working.
Margins: 17% → 23% by 2005
The engine of discovery
For decades, 3M's product development thrived on unpredictability. Variation was not a problem to be solved; it was the engine of discovery. When Six Sigma was applied to R&D, new product launches dropped sharply. The same logic that eliminated defects in manufacturing also eliminated the creative accidents that led to breakthroughs.

Surprises are not welcome
Six Sigma is built on the idea of bringing process variation under control, managing operations so that outputs stay within a specific, narrow range. Surprises are not welcome. Accidents are, by design, nearly impossible.
A commitment to failure tolerance
3M's "30/5 objective" was a cultural commitment to constant experimentation and a tolerance for failure. Thirty percent of sales had to come from products less than five years old, keeping the pipeline full of new ideas, many of which would not work out. Six Sigma's drive to eliminate errors ran directly counter to that.

Valuable in the right place
Over time, Six Sigma aims to shrink variance further still, making processes ever more reliable and their outputs ever more foreseeable. In 3M's manufacturing operations, this was genuinely valuable. The problem was that the same logic was applied everywhere.
Happy accidents, by design
Some of 3M's most celebrated innovations, including Post-it Notes, were happy accidents. They came from unexpected results that someone had the curiosity and freedom to follow. Six Sigma's goal of keeping outcomes within a predictable range meant eliminating the very conditions that made those moments possible.

The Tension The tools that make organizations more efficient are often the same tools that make them less innovative. Managing this tension is one of the central challenges of leading innovation.
