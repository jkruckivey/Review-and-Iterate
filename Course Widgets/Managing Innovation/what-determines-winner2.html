Ten factors that influence which design becomes dominant

Marketing Efforts
Firms invest in persuading customers, partners, and the public to favour their design. Heavy marketing can create awareness and preference before the market has settled, tipping adoption in one direction early on.

Functional Superiority
A design that performs better on the dimensions customers care about has an advantage. But this factor alone rarely determines the outcome. Many technically superior designs have lost to inferior ones when other factors were misaligned.

Network Effects
Some designs become more valuable as more people adopt them. A fax machine is useless if no one else has one. As one standard gains users, it becomes increasingly attractive relative to alternatives, creating a self-reinforcing cycle.

Switching Costs
Once users invest time, money, or habits into a design, the cost of switching to an alternative rises. High switching costs reduce the likelihood of displacement even when a superior alternative exists.

Market Power
A firm with significant market power can push its preferred design toward dominance through pricing, distribution control, or simply the weight of its existing customer relationships. The QWERTY story is partly a story about Remington's market power.

Timing and First-Mover Advantage
Being first to market allows a firm to establish user habits, build infrastructure, and accumulate adoption before competitors arrive. Early adoption can snowball into lock-in before alternatives have had a chance to prove themselves.

Alliances and Partnerships
Firms that successfully recruit partners, suppliers, and distributors to support their design can accelerate adoption and outmanoeuvre technically superior rivals. Standards battles are often won or lost in the boardroom as much as in the market.

Past Infrastructure Commitments

Past Infrastructure Commitments
Existing infrastructure built around an older standard can make it easier for a new design that is compatible with it to gain adoption. A design that requires entirely new infrastructure faces a much higher barrier, regardless of its merits.

Complementary Business Models
When firms in adjacent markets adopt a compatible standard, they drive broader adoption. The choices of video rental stores and the adult film industry played a significant role in the VHS versus Betamax outcome, tipping the balance well beyond what either format's technical merits could explain.

Government Edicts or Regulations
Governments sometimes declare a winning standard directly. In the medical records industry, some governments have required all systems to be compatible with one another, effectively mandating a dominant design and bypassing market competition entirely.
