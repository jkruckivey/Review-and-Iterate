The Legend of PARC

Business is full of similar examples. One of the most famous ones is Xerox Palo Alto Research Center, or PARC in the early 1970s. The amazing people at PARC -- writer Michael Hiltzig compared them to Norse gods in his excellent book, Dealers of Lightning -- developed many, many important innovation that have fueled the information revolution. They developed one of the first object-oriented software languages, SmallTalk, the concept for the laptop computer, the ethernet local area network, and even an early version of a computer very much like Apple's Macintosh -- only ten years earlier. But the mind-blowing innovations of the wizards at PARC were too much for the straightlaced corporate types at Xerox headquarters back in Connecticut. Executives at Xerox headquarters pretty much shut down PARC by 1975. Most of the innovations developed there were commercialized elsewhere. The only PARC innovation that was huge for Xerox was the laser printer, which paid off so well that it paid back their total investment in PARC many times over.

But if you think about it, the laser printer is the thing PARC invented that required the least imagination from Xerox execs. It was the invention that looked the most like a Xerox copier.
