Where Is It on the Curve?

Place each innovation on Rogers' S-curve and identify which adopter group is currently driving uptake
Question 1 of 5
Product 1
Electric Vehicles
EVs have moved well beyond the early enthusiast stage. Major manufacturers now offer dozens of models at a range of price points, charging infrastructure is expanding rapidly, and government incentives have accelerated purchase decisions. Sales are growing but still represent a minority of total vehicle purchases in most markets.
Which adopter group is currently driving EV uptake?
Product 2
Generative AI Tools (e.g. ChatGPT, Claude)
Generative AI tools burst into public awareness in late 2022 and have seen explosive growth since. Millions of people now use them regularly for writing, coding, and research. Businesses are integrating them into workflows at speed, and the tools are becoming embedded in products people already use. Adoption is accelerating rapidly across a broad population.
Which adopter group is currently driving generative AI uptake?
Product 3
Smart Home Devices (e.g. smart speakers, thermostats)
Smart home devices have been on the market for over a decade. Products like smart speakers and connected thermostats are now widely available and well-known, with significant penetration in many households. However, uptake has been uneven: enthusiastic in some segments, resistant in others. Growth has slowed compared to the early surge and the market is beginning to plateau in some categories.
Which adopter group is currently driving smart home device uptake?
Product 4
Augmented Reality Glasses
AR glasses have been promised as a transformative technology for years. Products exist, but they remain expensive, niche, and unfamiliar to most consumers. Mainstream use cases are still unclear to the average buyer, and the products have yet to find a beachhead segment that could serve as a reliable reference for broader adoption. The technology is compelling to enthusiasts but has not crossed into everyday life.
Which adopter group is currently engaging with AR glasses?
Product 5
3D Television
3D TVs launched with enormous fanfare in the early 2010s, promising a cinema-like experience at home. Early adopters were enthusiastic, and major manufacturers invested heavily in marketing. But mainstream consumers never followed. The glasses required were cumbersome, content was limited, and most people could not identify a compelling reason to switch from their existing flat screens. By the mid-2010s, manufacturers had quietly stopped producing them.
Where on the S-curve did 3D television ultimately end up?

Keep in Mind
Placing an innovation on the S-curve is rarely clear-cut. The same product can sit at different points for different markets or segments. The value of the exercise is in the reasoning, not just the answer.
