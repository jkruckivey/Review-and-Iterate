Champions Strategy: Partner

Your core coalition. Champions see transformation as their win — it solves a problem they care about. Involve them early in shaping strategy, not just executing it. When people co-create, they become invested in success.

Characteristics
  • High organizational influence and credibility
  • Genuine belief in the transformation vision
  • Willing to advocate when you're not in the room
  • Often motivated by aligned personal interests
Engagement Approach
  • Give ownership of specific workstreams
  • Showcase contributions publicly to reinforce commitment
  • Involve in decision-making, not just execution
  • Frame the transformation as their win
Course Example

When Adobe transitioned to Creative Cloud, they recruited product managers as champions by giving them authority to design new cloud-enabled features. Product managers became fierce advocates because the transformation empowered them to innovate.

Resistors Strategy: Engage

High-influence resistors can block transformation entirely. Dismissing them as "blockers" and working around them creates enemies who sabotage quietly. Effective leaders engage directly: understand why they resist, then address root causes.

Root Causes of Resistance
  • Fear of loss: job security, status, expertise becoming obsolete
  • Lack of trust: previous failed initiatives, skepticism about leadership
  • Information gap: don't understand the vision or their role in it
  • Legitimate concerns: they see risks leadership hasn't considered
Engagement Approach
  • Hold 1-on-1 conversations before drawing conclusions
  • Ask: "What concerns you? What would need to be true to support this?"
  • Listen genuinely — resistors often surface valid risks
  • Incorporate valid concerns to strengthen the plan
Course Example

A healthcare system launching AI diagnostics faced resistance from radiologists fearing job displacement. Leadership reframed: "AI handles routine scans; you focus on complex cases requiring expertise." Involving radiologists in designing AI integration turned resistance into ownership.

Advocates Strategy: Leverage

Advocates are enthusiastic supporters who lack formal authority. They may not be in the room where decisions are made, but they influence peers. Amplifying their voices multiplies the coalition's reach without requiring additional political capital.

Characteristics
  • High enthusiasm for the transformation vision
  • Limited formal authority or organizational seniority
  • Credible with peers at their level
  • Often frontline workers or mid-level contributors
Engagement Approach
  • Connect them with champions who can amplify their voice
  • Include in cross-functional working groups
  • Give them platforms: town halls, internal communications
  • Recognize contributions publicly to sustain motivation
Course Example

Frontline service technicians who are excited about predictive maintenance tools can be powerful peer influencers. Connecting them with the CTO champion and featuring their experiences in internal communications spreads genuine enthusiasm without top-down pressure.

Monitors Strategy: Watch

Monitors are low priority for intensive engagement. Keep them informed through standard communication channels. Their stance may shift as transformation progresses — visible wins can convert passive observers into advocates over time.

Characteristics
  • Low organizational influence on the transformation
  • Not actively opposing or supporting the initiative
  • May become more relevant as the transformation scales
  • Stance is often driven by what they observe happening elsewhere
Engagement Approach
  • Keep informed through standard communications
  • Don't over-invest political capital here
  • Monitor for shifts — a change in stance signals something worth noting
  • Quick wins and visible momentum can move them toward support
Course Example

A Finance team concerned about AI ROI but not directly involved in implementation sits in this quadrant. Keep them updated on business case developments. As pilots demonstrate results, their stance will naturally shift — and their influence may increase as budget decisions approach.

Build from partners first — then engage resistors directly. Your most dangerous mistake is ignoring high-influence resistors and hoping they stay quiet.
