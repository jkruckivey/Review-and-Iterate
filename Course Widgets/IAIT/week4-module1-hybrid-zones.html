Inner Zone

Bureaucratic Core

Stable operations governed by standardized processes

Where it applies Finance Human Resources Legal Compliance
Cultural Logic
Authority derives from formal position and established procedures. Success means executing processes correctly and consistently.
Why apply here
When errors have severe consequences and predictability matters most. Standardized processes ensure compliance and protect the organization.
Key strengths
  • Consistency and reliability across large-scale operations
  • Regulatory compliance and legal protection
  • Clear accountability through defined roles
Key challenge
Bureaucratic inertia slows adaptation. Updating procedures is itself bureaucratic, creating resistance to change.
Middle Zone

Meritocratic Middle

Performance-driven business units competing in established markets

Where it applies Business Units Sales Consulting Divisions Competitive Markets
Cultural Logic
Authority derives from demonstrated expertise and results. Success means achieving measurable performance targets.
Why apply here
When measurable outcomes and individual excellence create competitive advantage. Performance incentives drive accountability and results.
Key strengths
  • High achievement through clear performance standards
  • Talent retention via competitive incentives
  • Data-driven resource allocation
Key challenge
Performance metrics reward short-term results over long-term bets. Individual incentives can create knowledge hoarding and resistance to tools that threaten established expertise.
Outer Zone

Adhocratic Edge

Innovation teams exploring new technologies and business models

Where it applies R&D Innovation Labs New Ventures Digital Pilots
Cultural Logic
Authority derives from situational expertise for the problem at hand. Success means creating novel solutions through rapid experimentation.
Why apply here
When the problem is novel and speed of learning matters more than consistency. Fluid teams can pivot quickly as new information emerges.
Key strengths
  • Rapid adaptation and disruptive thinking
  • Innovation speed through experimentation culture
  • Cross-functional collaboration breaks down silos
Key challenge
Scaling and integration: what works at 50 people breaks at 500. Successful innovations must eventually connect back to core operations.

The design question is not "which model should we adopt?" but rather: which model should govern each part of the organization, given the work being done and the transformation goals being pursued?
